

1. Great Concept, Bad Market, Bad Business Model
Example: AI-Powered Personal Dream Analyzer

Concept: 9/10

Interesting product.
AI could analyze dream journals and identify patterns.
Technically feasible.
Easy to explain.

Market: 2/10

Most people don't care enough.
Low urgency problem.
Very few people actively seek solutions.

Business Model: 2/10

Hard to charge meaningful money.
Low retention.
Customer acquisition likely costs more than lifetime value.

Lesson:
You can build something cool that almost nobody needs.

2. Great Market, Bad Concept, Bad Business Model
Example: "Another To-Do List App"

Market: 9/10

Hundreds of millions of knowledge workers.
Massive productivity software market.
Customers already spend money.

Concept: 2/10

Little differentiation.
Doesn't solve a new problem.
Incremental improvement at best.

Business Model: 3/10

Difficult to charge because alternatives are abundant.
Extremely competitive.
High switching costs from incumbents.

Lesson:
A huge market doesn't automatically create opportunity.

3. Great Business Model, Bad Market, Bad Concept
Example: Premium Subscription for Daily Inspirational Quotes

Business Model: 8/10

Software margins near 100%.
Subscription revenue.
No delivery costs.
Scales infinitely.

Concept: 2/10

Weak value proposition.
Easy to copy.

Market: 2/10

Very few people willing to pay.
Low pain level.

Lesson:
A theoretically beautiful business model is worthless if nobody wants the product.

4. Great Concept + Great Market, Bad Business Model
Example: Free AI Startup Advisor

Concept: 8/10

Founders want advice.
AI can provide value.

Market: 8/10

Millions of entrepreneurs.

Business Model: 2/10

High AI costs.
Users unwilling to pay enough.
Difficult monetization.

This is where many AI startups die.

5. Great Concept + Great Business Model, Bad Market
Example: Software for Professional Typewriter Repair Shops

Concept: 8/10

Solves a real operational problem.

Business Model: 8/10

SaaS subscription.
High retention.

Market: 1/10

Almost no customers exist.

This is a classic "tiny niche" problem.

6. Great Market + Great Business Model, Bad Concept
Example: Yet Another CRM

Market: 10/10

Huge spending.

Business Model: 9/10

SaaS subscriptions.
High LTV.

Concept: 2/10

No meaningful differentiation.

Many founders enter attractive markets but can't answer: "Why this instead of the 50 existing options?"

7. Great Concept + Great Market + Great Business Model
Example: Early Calendly

Concept: 9/10

Eliminated scheduling back-and-forth.

Market: 9/10

Anyone who schedules meetings.

Business Model: 9/10

Freemium.
Viral distribution.
Low service cost.
Clear upgrade path.

8. My favorite example: Luxury Social Network for Pet Rocks

Concept: 1/10

The product itself isn't compelling.
Pet rocks were a novelty fad decades ago.
A social network specifically for pet rock owners creates very little value.
Even if built perfectly, users don't gain much from it.

Market: 1/10

Almost nobody owns pet rocks.
Even fewer are passionate enough to join a community.
The addressable market is tiny and unlikely to grow.

Business Model: 1/10

Hard to charge subscriptions.
Advertisers don't care about such a niche audience.
Merchandise revenue would be minimal.
Customer acquisition would cost more than customers are worth.
